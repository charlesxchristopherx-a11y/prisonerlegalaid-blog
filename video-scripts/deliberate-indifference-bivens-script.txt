"The Right to Medical Care in Federal Prison — And Why Your Lawsuit Options Just Changed"
Target length: 90–120 seconds (≈180–220 words at natural pace)

If you're in federal prison and you're not getting the medical care you need, you have a constitutional right to adequate care. But here's what almost nobody tells you: the way that right used to get enforced has quietly disappeared.

There are two different legal claims people confuse constantly. "Deliberate indifference" means a prison official actually knew about a serious risk to your health — and ignored it anyway. That's a very high bar. "Medical negligence" is different — it just means the care didn't meet the accepted standard, whether or not anyone knew.

For decades, federal prisoners could sue individual officials directly for deliberate indifference under something called a Bivens claim. Not anymore. In 2022, the Supreme Court's decision in Egbert versus Boule called creating these claims "disfavored." And in 2025 — just last year — the Court applied that same logic directly to a federal prisoner in Goldey versus Fields, and shut the door even further.

So what's left? The Federal Tort Claims Act. It's a different legal theory — ordinary negligence against the United States itself, not a Bivens claim against an individual official — but it's still very much alive. It usually needs expert testimony, and it comes with a strict two-year deadline to file.

If you or someone you love is dealing with denied or delayed care in federal prison, knowing which claim actually fits your situation matters. That's what we do.
