Every person in custody — pretrial or convicted, state or federal — has a constitutional right to adequate medical care. For convicted prisoners, that right comes from the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment; for pretrial detainees, courts generally apply a closely related standard under the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause. But having a constitutional right is different from having a working way to enforce it — and for federal prisoners specifically, the enforcement mechanism has quietly collapsed over the past several years.

## Deliberate indifference vs. medical negligence: two different claims

The Supreme Court established the governing constitutional standard in **Estelle v. Gamble**, 429 U.S. 97 (1976): inadequate medical care violates the Eighth Amendment only when it rises to "deliberate indifference to serious medical needs." That standard has two parts, later sharpened in **Farmer v. Brennan**, 511 U.S. 825 (1994) — a case that arose from a federal prisoner's own lawsuit:

1. An **objectively serious** medical need, and
2. Proof that the official **actually knew** of an excessive risk to health or safety and **disregarded** it anyway — a genuinely subjective standard, not just a "should have known" one.

**Medical negligence is a different legal question entirely.** It asks whether a provider met the accepted standard of care — not whether they knowingly ignored a known risk. A missed diagnosis, a delayed referral, or a genuine medical misjudgment can be negligence without ever coming close to deliberate indifference. This distinction determines which legal claim — if any — actually fits a given set of facts, and it's one of the most common points of confusion in this entire area.

## For federal prisoners, deliberate indifference used to mean a Bivens claim

In **Carlson v. Green**, 446 U.S. 14 (1980), the Supreme Court held that a federal prisoner's estate could bring a *Bivens* damages action directly against individual federal officials for an Eighth Amendment medical-neglect claim — and, notably, that the mere existence of an FTCA remedy on the same facts didn't block it. The Court reasoned that Bivens and the FTCA were meant to work as **parallel remedies**, with Bivens actually offering real advantages FTCA lacks: individual officer liability, the possibility of punitive damages, a jury trial, and a uniform federal standard rather than one that shifts with state law.

That was 1980. It is not where the law stands today.

## The Supreme Court has spent the last decade closing that door

Starting with **Ziglar v. Abbasi**, 582 U.S. 120 (2017), the Court built an increasingly restrictive two-step test for any Bivens claim that doesn't fit squarely within one of its three original recognized contexts: first, does the case present a "new Bivens context"? If so, do "special factors" suggest Congress — not the courts — should decide whether a damages remedy exists?

In **Egbert v. Boule**, 596 U.S. 482 (2022), the Court sharpened that test into something close to a one-way ratchet: *if there is even a single reason to hesitate, a court must refuse to recognize a Bivens remedy.* The Court went further, describing the creation of any new Bivens cause of action as "a disfavored judicial activity," and stated plainly that if it were deciding Bivens itself today, it would likely reject the whole doctrine.

Then, in **Goldey v. Fields**, 606 U.S. 942 (2025), the Court applied that same hostility directly to a federal prisoner. Fields, held in solitary confinement, alleged he was physically abused by prison officials during routine checks and sued under the Eighth Amendment. The Fourth Circuit let the claim proceed. The Supreme Court reversed — citing *Egbert* — and held that Bivens does not extend to an Eighth Amendment excessive-force claim against federal prison officials. The Court's reasoning tracks squarely onto medical-neglect claims too: Congress has legislated extensively on prisoner litigation (most notably the PLRA) without ever creating a damages remedy, extending Bivens risks interfering with prison administration, and federal prisoners already have alternative remedial structures available — even if those alternatives don't include an individual damages suit.

**The practical result:** Carlson v. Green has not been overruled, but the room to bring a *new* Bivens medical-neglect claim that doesn't match its exact 1980 facts is now vanishingly small. Federal courts applying Goldey and Egbert are very likely to dismiss new Bivens claims from federal prisoners as presenting a "new context" with "special factors" counseling against relief — which, per Egbert, is nearly always enough to end the case.

## What's still available: FTCA negligence claims

This is where the distinction between deliberate indifference and negligence stops being academic. The Federal Tort Claims Act — 28 U.S.C. §§ 1346(b), 2671–2680 — allows negligence claims against the United States itself for the negligent acts of federal employees, including medical staff, acting within the scope of their employment. Unlike a Bivens claim, an FTCA claim doesn't require proving anyone knowingly disregarded a risk — ordinary negligence, judged against the accepted standard of care, is enough.

That accessibility comes with its own real requirements. FTCA medical-negligence claims generally require **expert witness testimony** to establish what the standard of care was and how it was breached — courts don't assume jurors know what a reasonably competent provider would have done. And FTCA claims still require an administrative claim (Standard Form 95) stating a specific dollar amount, filed within two years of the incident, before any lawsuit can even begin.

## The bottom line

For a federal prisoner today, a case that would once have been framed as "deliberate indifference under Bivens" often has to be reframed as "negligence under the FTCA" instead — a real, available path, but a different legal theory with a different procedural roadmap and its own strict deadlines.
