Before 2018, only the Bureau of Prisons could ask a court to reduce a federal sentence for compassionate release — and it rarely did. The First Step Act changed that: federal prisoners can now file these motions directly with the sentencing court themselves. That single change has made compassionate release one of the most active areas of federal post-conviction practice.

## The legal standard

18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(A) allows a court to reduce a sentence if it finds "extraordinary and compelling reasons" warrant it — and only after considering whether a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements from the U.S. Sentencing Commission, and after weighing the sentencing factors in 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a) (the same factors a judge considers at original sentencing).

That last part matters: even a genuinely extraordinary and compelling circumstance doesn't guarantee release. The court still has to weigh public safety, the seriousness of the offense, and the purposes of sentencing before granting a reduction. This is a discretionary remedy, not an automatic one.

## What can qualify as "extraordinary and compelling"

The Sentencing Commission's policy statement identifies several categories courts commonly rely on:

- **Terminal illness** — a serious and advanced illness with an end-of-life trajectory
- **Serious medical conditions** that substantially diminish the ability to provide self-care in a correctional setting and are not expected to improve
- **Advanced age**, combined with a serious deterioration in physical or mental health tied to aging, and a substantial portion of the sentence already served
- **Family circumstances** — most commonly, the death or incapacitation of the only available caregiver for the prisoner's minor child, or an incapacitated spouse/partner requiring caregiving
- Other circumstances that, alone or combined, are of similar gravity to the categories above

## You have to exhaust first

Before filing with the court, you generally have to first submit a request to the warden and either exhaust the BOP's administrative appeal process, or wait 30 days from the warden's receipt of the request — whichever comes first. Filing with the court before satisfying this requirement is one of the most common reasons compassionate release motions get denied on procedural grounds before a judge ever reaches the merits.

## Why the motion itself matters so much

Because this is a discretionary remedy weighed against the § 3553(a) factors, the strength of the motion — how clearly it documents the medical or family circumstances, how it's supported by records, and how it addresses public safety and the sentencing factors head-on — genuinely affects the outcome. A thin motion listing a diagnosis without documentation, or one that doesn't grapple with the § 3553(a) factors at all, is an easy denial.
