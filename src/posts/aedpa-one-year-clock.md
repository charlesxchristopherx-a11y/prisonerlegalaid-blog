Federal habeas corpus is the last door in the courthouse — the proceeding where a federal judge reviews whether a conviction or sentence violates the Constitution. And it has a lock on it: a one-year statute of limitations that eliminates more petitions than any legal argument ever will.

## Where the year comes from

The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act (AEDPA) sets a one-year limitations period for habeas petitions — 28 U.S.C. § 2244(d) for state prisoners filing under § 2254, and 28 U.S.C. § 2255(f) for federal prisoners. File on day 366 and, absent an exception, the court can dismiss the petition without ever reaching your claims.

## When the clock starts

For most people, the year begins when the judgment becomes **final** — meaning direct review is over. Walk through your own timeline carefully:

- If you appealed and lost, and then sought review in the U.S. Supreme Court, finality comes when the Supreme Court denies or decides the petition.
- If you appealed and lost but did *not* go to the Supreme Court, finality generally comes when the time to file there (90 days) runs out.
- If you never appealed at all, finality comes when your time to file the direct appeal expired.

The statutes also list alternative start dates that apply in narrower situations — for example, when the factual basis of a claim could not have been discovered earlier with diligence, when an unconstitutional government impediment to filing is removed, or when the Supreme Court newly recognizes a right and makes it retroactive. Which trigger applies to which claim is exactly the kind of question worth getting help on *before* you file.

## What pauses the clock — and what doesn't

For state prisoners, § 2244(d)(2) stops the clock while a **properly filed** state post-conviction application is *pending*. Two traps hide in those words. First, "properly filed" means the state petition followed the state's own filing rules — an untimely state petition may not pause anything. Second, the clock is *paused*, not reset: whatever days ran before you filed in state court are already spent, and the clock resumes the moment state review ends.

And a mistake we see constantly: a federal habeas petition itself does not stop the clock while it's pending.
